# How to Choose a Cordless Drill for Home Projects

Buying a cordless drill should be simple — but with dozens of models across six major brands, most people end up confused. This guide breaks down every decision you need to make to choose the right drill for your home.

---

## Step 1: Decide How You'll Use It

Your use case determines everything else.

| Use Case | What You Need |
|---|---|
| Hanging pictures, light assembly | Any 12–18V drill with basic clutch |
| Furniture assembly, general home repair | 18–20V drill, brushless preferred |
| Decking, fencing, heavy driving | 18–20V, brushless, high torque |
| Drilling into concrete or masonry | **Hammer drill** or **rotary hammer** (not a standard drill) |
| Professional/contractor use | Milwaukee M18 FUEL or DEWALT DCD996 |

---

## Step 2: Choose Your Voltage

Voltage is the most visible spec, but don't overthink it:

- **12V:** Light tasks — compact, great for tight spaces and finish work. Not enough for driving long screws or boring large holes.
- **18V/20V MAX:** The standard for DIY and construction. Handles all home tasks. 20V MAX (DEWALT) = 18V (Makita/Ridgid/Ryobi) — same nominal voltage, different marketing.
- **60V+:** DEWALT FLEXVOLT / EGO — overkill for home use; designed for heavy-duty construction.

**For most homeowners: 18V/20V is the right choice.**

---

## Step 3: Brushless or Brushed Motor?

### Brushed Motor
- Lower cost
- More maintenance (brushes wear out eventually)
- Adequate for light, occasional use

### Brushless Motor
- **30–50% longer runtime** per charge
- Longer motor lifespan
- More consistent power delivery
- Better for regular use

**Recommendation:** If the price difference is under $30, always choose brushless.

## Step 5: Understand Clutch Settings

The clutch controls how much torque is applied before the drill disengages. This prevents:
- Over-driving screws through material
- Stripping screw heads
- Damaging the work surface

**How many positions do you need?**
- **10–15 positions:** Fine for most home tasks
- **20+ positions:** Better for precision work (cabinetry, furniture)
- **Drill mode (no clutch):** Required for boring holes — don't drive screws in this mode

---

## Step 6: Read the Key Specs

### UWO (Unit Watts Output)
True power measurement. Higher UWO = more powerful drill.
- Under 300 UWO: light duty
- 300–500 UWO: standard home use
- 500+ UWO: heavy duty

### Torque (In-Lbs)
How hard the drill twists. Higher = better for driving long screws and lag bolts.
- 300–500 in-lbs: home use
- 500–1,000+ in-lbs: construction

### Chuck Size
- **3/8-inch:** Common on compact drills — limited to smaller bits
- **1/2-inch:** Standard for full-size drills — fits all standard bit sizes

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Should I buy a kit or bare tool?**
Buy a kit if you're new to the platform — it includes batteries and a charger. Buy bare tools once you have batteries from other tools in the same platform.

**Do I need a separate impact driver?**
For most homeowners, a good drill handles both drilling and driving. An impact driver (bought later) is worth adding for heavy screw-driving tasks like decking and framing.

**What accessories do I need?**
Start with: a general-purpose bit set (Bosch or DEWALT titanium sets), a set of spade bits, a magnetic bit holder, and a countersink bit.
