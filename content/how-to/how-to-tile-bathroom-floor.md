# How to Tile a Bathroom Floor: Complete DIY Guide

Tiling a bathroom floor is one of the most rewarding home improvement projects — it transforms a space completely and adds lasting value. A standard 60 sq ft bathroom floor takes a weekend with the right preparation.

---

## What You'll Need

### Tools
- **Wet tile saw** — ([see our top picks](../best-tile-saws-home-use))
- Notched trowel (1/4 × 3/8 inch V-notch for most floor tile)
- Rubber float (for grouting)
- Tile spacers (1/8 inch for floor tile — adjust per look desired)
- Level (4-foot)
- Chalk line
- Tape measure and pencil
- Bucket and sponge
- Grout sponge (large cell)
- Knee pads

### Materials
- Floor tile (order 10–15% extra for cuts and waste)
- Thinset mortar (polymer-modified for best results)
- Tile spacers
- Grout (unsanded for joints under 1/8 inch; sanded for 1/8 inch+)
- Grout sealer
- Tile primer (for smooth concrete or existing tile)

---

## Step 1: Prepare the Subfloor

The subfloor must be:
- **Flat:** Use a level — no high or low spots over 3/16 inch in a 10-foot span
- **Solid:** No flex or bounce — add cement board or 3/4-inch plywood if needed
- **Clean and dry:** Remove all old adhesive, dust, and debris

For bathroom floors, install **cement board** (Hardiebacker or similar) over the plywood subfloor. Cement board is moisture-resistant and provides the ideal tile substrate.

1. Cut cement board to fit with a score-and-snap or circular saw with a carbide blade
2. Apply thinset to the subfloor first
3. Screw cement board every 8 inches using cement board screws
4. Tape joints with fiberglass mesh tape + thinset

---

## Step 2: Plan Your Layout

**Never start from a wall.** Walls are rarely perfectly square. Starting from the wall guarantees crooked tile lines.

**Find the center:**
1. Measure the width of the room and snap a chalk line down the center
2. Measure the length of the room and snap a chalk line across the center
3. You now have a cross at the room's center point

**Dry lay tiles** (no adhesive) from the center out to the walls:
- Lay a row of tiles with spacers along each chalk line
- See where the cut tiles fall at the walls — adjust the layout so no cut tile is less than half a tile wide at any wall
- Move your chalk lines by half a tile if needed to achieve balanced cuts

---

## Step 3: Mix Thinset

Follow manufacturer instructions exactly. Standard ratio is approximately 1 part water to 3 parts dry mix.

Mix until smooth — no lumps. The consistency should be like peanut butter: it holds a notch shape from the trowel but isn't stiff.

**Slake:** Let the mixed thinset rest for 5–10 minutes, then remix briefly. This improves workability.

Work in batches you can use in 30–45 minutes.

---

## Step 4: Set the Tiles

1. Spread thinset with the flat side of the trowel, then comb in one direction with the notched side
2. Start at the center cross — set your first tile precisely on the chalk lines
3. Press tiles firmly into the mortar with a slight twisting motion
4. Use spacers at all four corners
5. Check level frequently — use a rubber mallet to tap down high tiles
6. Maintain consistent grout joint width throughout

**Back-buttering:** For large tiles (12×12+), apply a thin layer of thinset to the tile back before setting. This ensures full coverage and prevents hollow spots.

**Coverage check:** Every few tiles, pull one up and check the mortar coverage on the back. You want 80%+ coverage. Adjust trowel angle if getting less.

Work in sections of 3–4 sq ft at a time. Avoid walking on freshly set tiles.

---

## Step 5: Cut Border Tiles

After the field tiles are set and cured (24 hours), measure and cut border tiles:

1. Measure each cut individually — rooms are rarely perfectly square
2. Mark cuts with a pencil or china marker
3. Cut with the wet tile saw — smooth, controlled feed rate
4. Check fit before setting — make adjustments as needed
5. Set cut tiles with thinset, maintaining consistent grout joints

---

## Step 6: Grout

Wait **24–48 hours** after setting before grouting. The thinset must cure fully.

1. Remove all tile spacers
2. Mix grout to a thick paste consistency
3. Apply grout with the rubber float, holding it at 45° to the tile surface
4. Push grout firmly into all joints with diagonal strokes
5. Remove excess grout from tile faces with the float edge
6. Wait 15–20 minutes until the grout haze begins to firm
7. Clean with a damp (not wet) grout sponge — circular motions first, then wipe across joints
8. Rinse sponge frequently — change water often
9. Repeat cleaning in 2–3 passes until tile faces are clear

**Final haze:** After 24 hours, buff remaining grout haze with a dry cloth or cheesecloth.

---

## Step 7: Seal the Grout

Wait **72 hours** after grouting before sealing.

Apply grout sealer with the included applicator or a small paintbrush:
1. Apply sealer along each grout joint
2. Wipe excess off tile faces immediately
3. Let dry per manufacturer instructions (typically 1–2 hours)
4. Apply a second coat for best protection

Re-seal annually for heavy-use bathrooms.

---

## Tips for Best Results

- **Don't rush the prep** — a flat, solid subfloor is 80% of a good tile job
- **Dry lay first** — always plan the layout before mixing any mortar
- **Clean trowel frequently** — old mortar on the trowel creates inconsistent ridges
- **Keep grout joints clean** — check for mortar in joints before it cures and is hard to remove
- **Use knee pads** — your knees will thank you
