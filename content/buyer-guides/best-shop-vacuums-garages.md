## Buyer Guide: What to Look For in a Garage Shop Vac

### Capacity: How Big Do You Need?
For most home garages, a 10-16 gallon tank is the sweet spot. Smaller 5-6 gallon units work well for quick cleanups, while larger 20+ gallon models are better suited to professional or heavy-use environments. Consider how often you are willing to stop and empty the tank — larger capacity means fewer interruptions.

### Peak HP vs. Real-World Suction
Peak horsepower figures are measured under specific lab conditions and do not always reflect real-world performance. Sealed suction (water lift) and CFM (cubic feet per minute) are more useful metrics. A higher water lift rating means better suction through clogged filters, while higher CFM means faster debris pickup in open conditions.

### Wet vs. Dry Capability
Most garage shop vacs are marketed as wet/dry units, but performance varies. Look for models with a dedicated foam filter sleeve for wet pickup — using a standard dry cartridge filter with liquid can damage the motor. The Ridgid WD1450 and Craftsman CMXEVBE17595 both handle wet messes reliably.

### Filtration and Dust Collection
If you do a lot of woodworking or drywall work in your garage, fine dust filtration matters. Look for units with HEPA filters or high-efficiency cartridges. Fine dust that bypasses the filter ends up recirculating into your workspace. Pairing your shop vac with a dust separator bucket can dramatically extend filter life.

### Noise Level
Shop vacuums are notoriously loud. If you work in an attached garage or near living spaces, consider the DEWALT DXV10P or other noise-reduced models. Some units run at 70 dB or less, which is noticeably more comfortable during extended use.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What size shop vac do I need for a garage?**
For most homeowners, a 10-16 gallon unit is ideal. It provides enough capacity for larger jobs without being too bulky to store. If your garage is small or you mainly do light cleanups, a 5-6 gallon model is sufficient.

**Can I use a shop vac to pick up water?**
Yes — wet/dry shop vacs are designed to handle liquids. Just make sure to remove the dry cartridge filter first and install a foam sleeve, or you risk damaging the filter. The tank should be emptied promptly after wet use to prevent mold.

**How do I reduce dust when using a shop vac?**
Use a high-efficiency or HEPA filter, and consider adding a dust separator between the hose and the tank. This pre-separates heavy debris before it reaches the filter, keeping suction strong and filters cleaner longer.

**Are shop vacs loud?**
Most shop vacs run between 75-85 dB, which is similar to a lawnmower. Premium models like the DEWALT DXV10P are engineered to run quieter, around 70 dB or less. Hearing protection is a good idea during extended use.
