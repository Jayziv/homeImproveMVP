## Buyer Guide: What to Look For in a Random Orbital Sander

### Corded vs. Cordless
Corded sanders run at consistent speed regardless of battery charge and are lighter since there is no battery weight. Cordless models offer freedom of movement — great for sanding furniture in the living room or decking outdoors. For workshop use at a bench, corded is generally preferred. For sanding around the house, cordless is more convenient.

### OPM (Orbits Per Minute) and Variable Speed
OPM determines how aggressively the sander removes material. Higher OPM removes material faster; lower OPM gives more control for fine finishing. Variable speed lets you match the OPM to the task — the Makita BO5041K, Bosch ROS20VSC, and WEN 6301 all offer variable speed, which is especially useful for delicate surfaces like veneer or painted furniture.

### Dust Collection: Bags vs. Vacuum Port
Most random orbital sanders include a dustbag, but connecting directly to a shop vac via the dust port is much more effective. Look for sanders with both options — a dustbag for quick jobs and a vacuum port for serious dust control. The Bosch ROS20VSC microfilter canister is particularly effective at capturing fine particles.

### Pad Size: 5 inches is Standard
Most home-use random orbital sanders use 5-inch hook-and-loop pads. Sanding discs in this size are widely available in every grit from 40 to 400. Stick with 5-inch models for maximum disc selection and compatibility.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What grit sandpaper should I start with?**
For rough material removal or paint stripping, start with 60-80 grit. For smoothing bare wood before staining, use 120-150 grit. For final finish sanding before applying a topcoat, use 180-220 grit. Always sand progressively through the grits — do not skip more than one step.

**Can I use a random orbital sander on drywall?**
Yes, but use a fine grit (120-150) and a vacuum port connection to control the extremely fine dust. Drywall dust is a respiratory hazard — always wear a dust mask and provide ventilation.

**What is the difference between a random orbital sander and an orbital sander?**
A random orbital sander moves in a random, oscillating pattern that prevents swirl marks. A standard orbital sander moves in a fixed circular orbit and can leave circular swirl marks visible in certain lighting. For woodworking and finishing, random orbital sanders are strongly preferred.

**How do I prevent swirl marks with a random orbital sander?**
Use a light touch — let the weight of the sander do the work. Do not press down hard, and keep the sander moving slowly across the surface. Finish with a fine grit (180-220) before applying any stain or topcoat.
