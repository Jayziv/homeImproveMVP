## Buyer Guide: What to Look For in a Beginner Jigsaw

### Corded vs. Cordless for Beginners
For beginners, a corded jigsaw has the advantage of consistent power and lighter weight (no battery). Cordless models offer freedom of movement and no trip hazard — particularly useful when working on large sheets of plywood on the floor. Either option works well for learning; choose based on whether you already own compatible batteries.

### Orbital Action: Why It Matters
Orbital action moves the blade in an oval pattern rather than straight up and down, allowing faster cuts in wood. Most jigsaws offer 2-4 orbital settings — lower settings are better for fine cuts and curves, higher settings cut faster in straight lines. As a beginner, start with no orbital action and increase it as you get comfortable.

### Variable Speed: Essential for Beginners
Variable speed lets you start cuts slowly and build speed as confidence grows. It also allows you to match the cutting speed to the material — slower for metal, faster for wood. All five jigsaws on this list offer variable speed control.

### Blade Selection for Beginners
Start with a standard T-shank general-purpose wood blade (10-12 TPI). T-shank blades are universal and fit all modern jigsaws. For cutting curves or intricate shapes, use a narrower blade. For cutting plywood cleanly with minimal tear-out, use a fine-tooth blade (20+ TPI).

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is a jigsaw hard to use for beginners?**
No — jigsaws are among the most beginner-friendly power saws. The blade cuts slowly enough to control, and the saw is lightweight and easy to guide. Start with straight cuts, then work up to curves as your confidence grows.

**What wood thickness can a jigsaw cut?**
Most jigsaws cut up to 5-1/2 inches in wood with the right blade. For beginners, focus on cutting 3/4 inch plywood, 2x4 lumber, and similar dimensions. Thicker materials require slower feed rates and sharp blades.

**Can a jigsaw cut curves?**
Yes — cutting curves is one of the jigsaw primary strengths. Tighter curves require narrower blades and slower feed speeds. Practice on scrap wood before cutting your actual project pieces.

**What is the difference between T-shank and U-shank jigsaw blades?**
T-shank blades have a tang at the top that clicks into a quick-release chuck — these are the modern standard and fit all five jigsaws on this list. U-shank blades are older and require a set screw to install. Always check your jigsaw accepts T-shank blades before buying blade packs.
