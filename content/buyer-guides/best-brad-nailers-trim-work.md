## Buyer Guide: What to Look For in a Brad Nailer

### Cordless vs. Pneumatic
Cordless brad nailers offer unmatched portability — no hose, no compressor, no dragging equipment from room to room. They are ideal for finish work throughout the house. Pneumatic nailers are lighter, more powerful, and less expensive, but require an air compressor. For frequent use, pneumatic tools are often preferred by professionals. For occasional home projects, cordless wins on convenience.

### 18-Gauge vs. 16-Gauge
18-gauge brad nailers are ideal for trim work — they leave a smaller hole that is easier to fill and nearly invisible after painting. 16-gauge finish nailers are better for structural trim and thicker stock but leave a larger hole. For baseboards, door casings, and crown molding, 18-gauge is almost always the right choice.

### Depth Adjustment
Look for tool-free depth adjustment — it lets you dial in the nail depth quickly without carrying a screwdriver. This is especially important when working with different wood species or thicknesses. All five nailers on this list offer this feature.

### Magazine Capacity
Most 18-gauge nailers hold 100-110 nails per magazine. Top-load magazines are common and easy to reload, while sequential-only models require more deliberate trigger pulls for precision placement.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is the difference between a brad nailer and a finish nailer?**
Brad nailers use 18-gauge nails with a very small head, leaving a nearly invisible hole ideal for delicate trim. Finish nailers use 15 or 16-gauge nails with a larger head for more holding power — better for thicker stock or structural applications but leaving a more noticeable hole.

**What length brad nails should I use for baseboards?**
For 3/4 in. baseboards, use 1-1/4 in. to 1-1/2 in. brad nails to ensure they penetrate into the wall stud or plate. For thinner trim like door stop molding, 1 in. nails are usually sufficient.

**Do I need a compressor for a cordless brad nailer?**
No — cordless brad nailers like the DEWALT DCN680D1 and Makita XNB01Z run entirely on battery power. However, pneumatic models like the WEN 61720 do require an air compressor. See our guide to the [best air compressors for home use](/best-air-compressors-home-use) for pairing recommendations.

**How do I fix a nail jam in a brad nailer?**
Most nailers have a quick-release nose or jam-clearing lever. Disconnect the power source first (unplug air line or remove battery), open the jam-clearing mechanism, and remove the jammed nail with pliers. Never fire the nailer into your hand to clear a jam.
