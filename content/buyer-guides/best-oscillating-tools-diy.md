## Buyer Guide: What to Look For in an Oscillating Multi-Tool

### Corded vs. Cordless
Cordless oscillating tools offer the freedom to work anywhere without tripping over a cord — particularly valuable for floor work, cutting door jambs, and working in closets or cabinets. Corded models offer unlimited runtime at consistent speed, making them better for extended, demanding use. For most DIY tasks, a cordless model is more practical.

### OPM Range and Variable Speed
OPM (oscillations per minute) determines how aggressively the tool cuts. Variable speed is essential — lower speeds (6,000-10,000 OPM) are better for sanding and polishing delicate surfaces, while higher speeds (15,000-20,000 OPM) cut through wood, metal, and drywall quickly. The Makita XMT03Z offers the widest OPM range at 6,000-20,000.

### Accessory Systems: Universal vs. Proprietary
Most modern oscillating tools accept universal accessories (the Makita XMT03Z accepts both). Bosch StarlockPlus and older systems may require specific accessories. Look for a tool that accepts the widest range of blades and attachments — standard plunge cut blades, sanding pads, and scraping blades are the most used.

### Vibration Control
Excessive vibration causes hand fatigue and can reduce cut accuracy. Tools with anti-vibration mounting systems (like the Bosch StarlockPlus system) and brushless motors (DEWALT DCS356C1) are noticeably smoother. If you plan to use your tool for extended sessions, vibration control matters.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What can an oscillating multi-tool cut?**
An oscillating tool cuts wood, metal, drywall, plastic, grout, and tile with the right blade. Plunge cut blades cut flush against surfaces — ideal for cutting door jambs. Bi-metal blades cut through nails, screws, and metal pipes. Sanding pads smooth wood and finishes in tight corners.

**Can an oscillating tool replace a circular saw?**
For straight cuts in lumber, no — an oscillating tool is much slower than a circular saw. But for cuts in confined spaces, flush cuts against floors or walls, or making plunge cuts, an oscillating tool is the only tool for the job.

**How do I cut a door jamb with an oscillating tool?**
Lay a piece of flooring sample against the jamb as a depth guide. Use a plunge-cut wood blade and cut horizontally at the base of the jamb, using the flooring sample as a guide for the tool base. This creates a clean undercut for new flooring to slide under.

**What accessories should I buy for an oscillating tool?**
Start with: a standard wood plunge cut blade, a bi-metal plunge cut blade (for nails and metal), an assorted sanding pad set (80, 120, 220 grit), and a rigid scraper blade for removing old caulk and adhesive. Most complete kits like the DEWALT DCS356C1 include all of these.
